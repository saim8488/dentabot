# What to Expect from a Root Canal

## Before the Procedure

Your dentist will take X-rays to assess the extent of infection and plan the treatment. You will be given local anesthesia to numb the area, ensuring you are comfortable throughout the procedure. If you feel anxious, sedation options are available.

## During the Procedure

1. A rubber dam (dental sheet) is placed around the tooth to keep it dry and isolated.
2. The dentist creates a small opening in the top of the tooth to access the pulp chamber.
3. Using tiny, specialized instruments, the infected or damaged pulp tissue is carefully removed.
4. The root canals are cleaned, shaped, and disinfected.
5. The canals are filled with gutta-percha, a biocompatible rubber-like material, and sealed.
6. A temporary filling is placed. A permanent crown is usually placed at a follow-up visit.

## Pain and Comfort

Contrary to common belief, modern root canal treatment is not painful. Most patients report that the procedure feels similar to getting a filling. The anesthesia ensures you feel little to no discomfort. After the procedure, mild soreness is normal and can be managed with over-the-counter pain medication.

## After the Procedure

- Avoid chewing on the treated tooth until the permanent crown is placed.
- Take any prescribed medications as directed.
- Mild discomfort or sensitivity may last a few days.
- Contact the clinic if you experience severe pain, swelling, or fever.

## Long-Term Outlook

A root canal-treated tooth can last a lifetime with proper care. A crown is typically recommended to protect and strengthen the tooth. Continue regular brushing, flossing, and dental visits to maintain your results.
